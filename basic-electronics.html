CHAPTER 01 — FUNDAMENTALS

Understanding Electric Circuits

An electric circuit is a closed loop through which electric current can flow. It requires a power source, conductors (wires), and a load (component that uses power). Understanding the four fundamental circuit types is the foundation of all electronics.

⚡ WHAT IS AN ELECTRIC CIRCUIT?

Electric current is the flow of electrons through a conductor. For this flow to happen, there must be a complete, unbroken path from the positive terminal of a power source, through the circuit components, and back to the negative terminal. This closed loop is what we call an electric circuit.

🔋 Voltage (V)
The electromotive force or "electrical pressure" that drives electrons through the circuit. Measured in Volts (V). Think of it as water pressure in a pipe.
⚡ Current (I)
The actual flow of electrons through the circuit. Measured in Amperes (A) or milliamperes (mA). Like the volume of water flowing per second.
🟤 Resistance (R)
Opposition to current flow. Measured in Ohms (Ω). Like the width of a pipe — narrower means more resistance, less flow.
💡 Ohm's Law
The fundamental law connecting all three: V = I × R. If you know any two values, you can calculate the third. This rule governs every circuit.

CIRCUIT TYPE 01

Open Circuit

An open circuit is an incomplete electrical path where current cannot flow. The circuit has a break or gap somewhere — caused by a switch in the OFF position, a blown fuse, a broken wire, or a disconnected component.

OPEN CIRCUIT ANIMATION
📖 WHAT IS AN OPEN CIRCUIT?

In an open circuit, the electrical path is intentionally or accidentally broken. Electrons cannot flow because there is no complete loop. The voltage source still has its potential, but without a closed path, no current flows.

I = 0 A   |   R = ∞ Ω
Current is zero. Resistance is theoretically infinite (open gap). Voltage across the source = 9V, but no power is consumed.
  • Open Switch: The most common intentional open circuit. Pressing or flipping a switch creates a break in the wire.
  • Blown Fuse: A fuse melts when too much current flows, deliberately creating an open to protect the circuit.
  • Broken Wire: Accidental break in a conductor — bent repeatedly, corroded, or physically cut.
  • Disconnected Component: A component leg not fully inserted in a breadboard creates a hidden open circuit.
💡 Detecting Open Circuits: Use a multimeter in continuity mode (beep test). No beep = open circuit. Or use voltage mode — measure voltage across the gap; the full source voltage appears there in an open circuit.
CIRCUIT TYPE 02

Closed Circuit

A closed circuit is a complete, unbroken electrical path allowing current to flow continuously from the power source, through the components, and back. Every working electronic device operates on the principle of a closed circuit.

CLOSED CIRCUIT — ELECTRONS FLOWING
📖 HOW A CLOSED CIRCUIT WORKS

In a closed circuit, electrons leave the negative terminal of the battery, travel through the wires and components, do work (light up LEDs, spin motors, produce sound), and return to the positive terminal. This continuous flow is current.

V = I × R   →   I = V ÷ R
With 9V source and 330Ω resistor: I = 9÷330 = 27.3mA
Power consumed: P = V×I = 9 × 0.0273 = 245mW
  • Power Source: Battery, power supply, or solar cell — provides the voltage that drives electrons.
  • Conductors (Wires): Low-resistance paths that allow electrons to move freely between components.
  • Load: Any component that uses electrical energy — LED, motor, buzzer, resistor.
  • Switch (optional): Allows you to intentionally open or close the circuit — control when current flows.
CIRCUIT TYPE 03

Series Circuit

In a series circuit, all components are connected end-to-end in a single loop. There is only one path for current to travel. The same current flows through every component, but the voltage divides proportionally across each one.

SERIES CIRCUIT — SAME CURRENT THROUGH ALL
📐 SERIES CIRCUIT RULES
Rtotal = R₁ + R₂ + R₃ + ...
Total resistance is the sum of all resistances. Current is same everywhere. Voltages add up to source voltage.
  • Single Current Path: All electrons must pass through every component — there is no other route. I is identical through every component.
  • Voltage Divides: Each resistor drops a portion of the total voltage. V₁/V₂ = R₁/R₂ — voltage drops are proportional to resistance.
  • Increasing Total Resistance: Adding more components in series always increases total resistance and decreases current.
  • Failure propagates: If any component in a series circuit fails open (breaks), the entire circuit stops — no current anywhere.
💡 Real Example: Old Christmas string lights were wired in series — when one bulb burnt out, ALL lights went dark. Modern lights use parallel wiring to avoid this problem.
CIRCUIT TYPE 04

Parallel Circuit

In a parallel circuit, components are connected across the same two nodes, creating multiple independent paths for current. Each branch operates independently — the same voltage appears across all branches, but current divides between them.

PARALLEL CIRCUIT — CURRENT SPLITS AT JUNCTIONS
📐 PARALLEL CIRCUIT RULES
1/Rtotal = 1/R₁ + 1/R₂ + 1/R₃
Total resistance is always LESS than the smallest branch resistance. For equal resistors: Rtotal = R/n (e.g., three 1kΩ = 333Ω)
  • Multiple Current Paths: Electrons can choose any branch. Current distributes based on resistance — more current flows through lower resistance paths.
  • Same Voltage: Every branch sees the exact same voltage — the source voltage appears fully across each branch.
  • Independent Operation: If one branch fails, the other branches continue working. This is why household wiring is parallel.
  • Increased Current Draw: Adding more parallel branches provides more paths, reducing total resistance and increasing total current from the source.
🏠 Real-World Parallel: Your home's electrical outlets are all wired in parallel. Each appliance gets 230V independently. Turning off one appliance (opening one branch) doesn't affect any others.

🔌 CALCULATING LED RESISTOR VALUE

Never connect an LED directly to a power supply without a series current-limiting resistor. Too much current destroys the LED instantly. The resistor limits current to a safe level.

R = (Vsupply − Vf) ÷ If
Vsupply=9V | Red LED Vf=2V | If=20mA=0.02A

🔊 ACTIVE vs PASSIVE BUZZER
🔔 Active Buzzer
Has an internal oscillator circuit. Just apply DC voltage → it buzzes automatically at its fixed frequency. Simpler to use — just connect + and −. Common in Arduino projects with digitalWrite(pin, HIGH). Look for a small sticker or sealed bottom.

⚡ VOLTAGE DIVIDER WITH LDR

Pair an LDR with a fixed resistor (e.g., 10kΩ) to create a voltage divider. In the dark, LDR has high resistance → voltage at the junction rises → triggers the circuit. COMPONENT 04

The Capacitor

A capacitor stores electrical energy in an electric field between two conductive plates separated by an insulating material (dielectric). It can charge up quickly and release that energy rapidly — essential for filtering, timing, and energy storage in circuits.

Capacitor
PASSIVE · ENERGY STORAGE · CAN BE POLARIZED
A capacitor stores charge on two conductive plates separated by a dielectric. When connected to a power source, it charges up; when disconnected and connected to a load, it releases stored energy. Capacitors block DC but pass AC — making them invaluable for filtering, coupling, and decoupling. Capacitance is measured in Farads (F).

📐 TYPES OF CAPACITORS
⚡ Electrolytic
Large capacitance (1µF–10,000µF). Polarized — must connect + to positive. Used for power supply filtering, bulk energy storage. Cylindrical shape with stripe marking the negative lead.
🔵 Ceramic (Disc)
Small capacitance (1pF–10µF). Non-polarized — any orientation. Used for high-frequency bypassing, decoupling near ICs. Very stable and durable. Yellow/beige disc shape.
🟡 Polyester Film
Medium capacitance (1nF–10µF). Non-polarized. Good tolerance and stability. Used in audio circuits, timing circuits, and filters where precision matters.
⚪ Tantalum
Compact electrolytic alternative (0.1µF–100µF). Polarized with + marked. Very stable. Used in surface-mount electronics. NEVER reverse-bias — can fail catastrophically.
τ = R × C  |  t ≈ 5τ to fully charge
RC time constant: with R=10kΩ and C=100µF → τ = 1 second. The capacitor reaches ~63% charge in 1τ, and ~99% in 5τ (5 seconds).
💡 Key Uses: Power supply filtering (removes AC ripple), signal coupling (pass AC, block DC), timer circuits (with resistors), bypass capacitors (stabilize supply voltage near ICs), and energy storage for camera flash or motor startup.
⚠️ Warning: Electrolytic capacitors are polarized. Connecting them backward or exceeding the voltage rating can cause them to explode or leak. Always check polarity — the longer lead is positive; the stripe marks negative.
COMPONENT 05

The Inductor

An inductor is a passive component that stores energy in a magnetic field when current flows through it. It consists of a coil of wire — sometimes wound around a ferromagnetic core. Inductors resist changes in current, making them vital for filtering, energy conversion, and oscillation circuits.

Inductor
PASSIVE · MAGNETIC ENERGY STORAGE · NON-POLARIZED
An inductor stores energy in a magnetic field created when current flows through its coil windings. It opposes changes in current — when current increases, it resists; when current decreases, it tries to maintain flow. This property (inductance, measured in Henries) is opposite to capacitance and is used in filters, transformers, and switching power supplies.

🌀 HOW INDUCTORS WORK

When current flows through a wire, it creates a magnetic field around it. Coiling the wire concentrates this field, creating a much stronger effect. When current changes, the magnetic field changes — and a changing magnetic field induces a voltage that opposes the current change (Lenz's Law).

🔊 RF Chokes
Block high-frequency signals (RF noise) while allowing DC and low-frequency current to pass. Used to keep power supply noise out of sensitive circuits.
🔄 Transformers
Two inductors magnetically coupled. Energy transfers between coils at different voltage/current ratios. Foundation of AC power distribution worldwide.
⚡ Boost Converters
Inductors store energy when a switch is closed and release it at higher voltage when the switch opens. Used in phone chargers, LED drivers, and DC-DC converters.
📡 LC Oscillators
Paired with a capacitor, an inductor creates a resonant "tank" circuit that oscillates at f = 1/(2π√LC). Used in radio transmitters and receivers to select frequency.
fresonant = 1 / (2π√LC)
LC resonant frequency — with L=100µH and C=10nF → f = 1/(2π×√(10⁻⁴×10⁻⁸)) ≈ 159 kHz. This is how radio tuning circuits work.
💡 Key Insight: Inductors are the "momentum keepers" of electronics — like a flywheel, they resist sudden changes. Capacitors resist voltage changes; inductors resist current changes. Together they form the building blocks of all filters and oscillators.
COMPONENT 06 — THE BUILDING BLOCK OF MODERN ELECTRONICS

Transistor BC547

The transistor is arguably the most important invention in electronics history. The BC547 is an NPN BJT (Bipolar Junction Transistor) — a tiny semiconductor device that can amplify weak signals or act as an electronically-controlled switch. Billions are used in every computer chip, phone, and electronic device.

BC547 NPN Transistor
ACTIVE · NPN BJT · 3-TERMINAL · AMPLIFIER/SWITCH
The BC547 has three terminals: Emitter (E), Base (B), and Collector (C). A small base current (µA) controls a much larger collector current (mA). With a current gain (hFE/β) of 100–800, it amplifies signals or acts as a switch — fully OFF when IB=0, fully ON (saturation) when IB is sufficient.

⚡ TWO OPERATING MODES
🔌 Switch Mode (Digital)
When IB is 0 → transistor is fully OFF (cutoff). Collector-Emitter acts like an open switch. When IB is sufficient (I_B ≥ I_C/hFE) → fully ON (saturation). Acts like a closed switch. Used to drive motors, relays, LEDs, buzzers from microcontroller pins.
📈 Amplifier Mode (Analog)
In the active region, I_C = β × I_B. A tiny base current variation controls a proportionally larger collector current. Used in audio amplifiers, signal amplifiers, and sensor circuits where small signals need boosting.
IC = β × IB  |  IE = IC + IB
With β=200 and I_B=50µA → I_C = 200×50µA = 10mA. The emitter current is the sum of collector and base currents.
💡 Pin Identification (BC547 TO-92 package): Hold the flat face toward you, leads pointing down. From left to right: Collector (C) — Base (B) — Emitter (E). The Emitter always points toward ground in NPN circuits. A base resistor (10kΩ–100kΩ) is required to limit base current.
🔑 Key Rule: NPN transistor turns ON when V_BE ≥ 0.6V (base is 0.6V more positive than emitter). Current flows from Collector to Emitter when ON. For switch applications, add a 1N4007 diode (flyback diode) across inductive loads like relays and motors to prevent voltage spikes.
PROJECT 01 — AUTOMATION

Smart Street Light

Build an automatic street light that turns ON when it gets dark and OFF in daylight — no manual switching required. This uses an LDR to sense light levels and a BC547 transistor to control the LED, demonstrating real-world sensor-actuator control.

PROJECT 01 LDR-Controlled Automatic LED Street Light BEGINNER

At the heart of this circuit is a voltage divider formed by the LDR and a 10kΩ resistor. In darkness, the LDR's resistance is very high (≥1MΩ), so more voltage appears at the base of the BC547, turning it ON and lighting the LED. In bright light, the LDR resistance drops (≤1kΩ), pulling the base voltage low — turning the transistor OFF and extinguishing the LED.

BC547 Transistor LDR (Photoresistor) 10kΩ Resistor 470Ω Resistor LED (any color) 9V Battery Breadboard + Wires
⚙️ HOW IT WORKS — STEP BY STEP
1
Dark Condition: LDR resistance jumps to 1MΩ+. In the voltage divider with the 10kΩ resistor, most of the 9V appears at the junction (≈8.5V). This high voltage is applied through the base resistor to the BC547 base.
2
Transistor Turns ON: With V_BE ≥ 0.6V, the BC547 enters saturation. Collector-Emitter resistance drops to nearly zero — current flows from 9V through 470Ω, through the LED, into the collector, out the emitter to GND.
3
Light Condition: LDR resistance falls to ~500Ω. Now the voltage divider output drops below 0.6V — the transistor turns OFF. No collector current → LED goes dark.
4
Sensitivity Adjustment: Replace the fixed 10kΩ with a 10kΩ potentiometer. Rotating it adjusts the switching threshold — you control exactly how dark it must get before the LED activates.
🌙 Real-World Use: This is exactly how commercial streetlights, garden path lights, and automatic security lights work — just with a relay instead of an LED, to switch mains voltage loads.
PROJECT 02 — TOUCH SENSING

Touch Sensitive Light

Build a touch-activated LED that turns on when you bridge two metal pads with your fingertip. Human skin conducts a tiny current — enough to trigger the base of a BC547 transistor. This demonstrates how living bodies can become part of an electrical circuit.

PROJECT 02 Human Touch Sensor using BC547 BEGINNER

The human body has a resistance of roughly 1kΩ–100kΩ between two points of contact. When you bridge the two touch pads, your body resistance allows a tiny current to flow into the transistor base. The BC547 amplifies this to control a much larger LED current. The 100kΩ base resistor limits maximum base current; skin resistance does the rest of the voltage division.

BC547 Transistor 100kΩ Resistor 470Ω Resistor LED 9V Battery 2× Metal Pins / Copper Tape Breadboard
⚙️ HOW IT WORKS
1
No Touch: No current path exists between pads A and B. Base of BC547 sees no current. Transistor is fully OFF. LED is dark.
2
Touch Applied: Your finger bridges pads A and B. Body resistance (~10kΩ–50kΩ) allows a tiny current to flow from the 9V supply through your skin into Pad B, through the 100kΩ resistor to the base.
3
Transistor Switches ON: V_BE exceeds 0.6V threshold. Collector current flows, energizing the LED. The transistor amplifies the tiny body current by a factor of β (100–800) to drive the LED.
4
Touch Released: Body current path breaks. V_BE drops below 0.6V. Transistor returns to cutoff. LED turns off immediately.
💡 Enhancement Ideas: Add a 1MΩ resistor between base and emitter to improve noise immunity. Use a relay instead of LED to switch higher-voltage loads. Add a 555 timer monostable to keep the LED on for a few seconds after touch is released (touch-activated latch).
PROJECT 03 — LEVEL SENSING

Water Level Indicator

Build a 3-level water indicator that visually shows the water level in a tank using three LEDs. Metal probes at different heights complete circuits through the water (which conducts electricity), triggering transistors at each level. This is used in water tanks, industrial liquid reservoirs, and flood warning systems.

PROJECT 03 3-Level Water Tank Indicator (Low / Mid / Full) INTERMEDIATE

Water is a weak conductor (especially tap water with dissolved minerals). When water rises to reach a probe, it completes the circuit from the common GND probe through the water to that level's transistor base — activating that LED. Each level uses an independent BC547 transistor and LED, so all three levels can show simultaneously, indicating exactly how full the tank is.

3× BC547 Transistors 3× LEDs (Red/Yellow/Green) 3× 470Ω Resistors (collector) 3× 10kΩ Resistors (base) 4× Metal Probes (wires) 9V Battery Breadboard
⚙️ HOW IT WORKS
1
Common GND Probe: One probe always touches the water and connects to GND. This is the common reference for all level sensors.
2
Low Level: Water reaches the lowest probe → conducts current through water resistance → base of first BC547 → Red LED ON (tank is low — refill soon).
3
Mid Level: Water reaches mid probe → second BC547 activates → Yellow LED ON alongside red (medium water level).
4
Full Level: Water reaches top probe → all three transistors ON → Green LED also glows (tank full — stop pumping).
🔧 Tip: Use stainless steel or gold-plated wires as probes to prevent corrosion. Add a buzzer to the "full" transistor output to alert when the tank is full. For cleaner water with higher resistance, reduce base resistors to 4.7kΩ for better sensitivity.
PROJECT 04 — OSCILLATION

Blinking LED Circuit

Build a self-oscillating astable multivibrator using two BC547 transistors, two capacitors, and resistors. The circuit alternately switches two transistors ON and OFF in a continuous cycle — producing a blinking pattern without any microcontroller or timer IC needed.

PROJECT 04 Astable Multivibrator — Dual LED Blinker INTERMEDIATE

An astable multivibrator has no stable state — it continuously oscillates between two quasi-stable states. When Q1 is ON, its collector (low voltage) holds Q2's base through C2, keeping Q2 OFF. Meanwhile C1 charges. When C1 is charged enough, Q2 turns ON, pulling Q1's base low — switching the states. This self-sustaining oscillation produces the blinking frequency: f ≈ 1/(1.38 × R × C).

2× BC547 Transistors (Q1, Q2) 2× 47µF Electrolytic Capacitors 2× 47kΩ Resistors (base) 2× 470Ω Resistors (collector) 2× LEDs (different colors) 9V Battery Breadboard
⚙️ THE OSCILLATION CYCLE
1
Q1 ON State: Q1 saturates → LED1 glows. Q1's collector is near 0V, which is coupled through C2 to Q2's base, holding Q2 in cutoff. LED2 is OFF. C1 charges through the 47kΩ resistor toward +9V.
2
State Switch: C1 charges until Q2's base reaches 0.6V. Q2 turns ON → its collector drops to 0V → this 0V is coupled back through C1 to Q1's base → Q1 turns OFF. LED1 goes dark, LED2 lights up.
3
Q2 ON State: C2 now charges in the opposite direction through the 47kΩ, until Q1's base again reaches 0.6V — switching states back. The cycle repeats indefinitely.
4
Frequency Control: Increase R (e.g., to 100kΩ) for slower blink. Increase C (e.g., to 100µF) for slower blink. Decrease both for faster blink. Use matched components on both sides for symmetric 50% duty cycle.
💡 Real Applications: This exact circuit is used in turn signal indicators (blinkers) in older vehicles, warning lights, heart rate indicators, and clock circuits. It's also the foundation for understanding 555 timer astable operation.
PROJECT 05 — ADVANCED

Wireless Power Transmission

Transmit electrical power through the air with no wires — using resonant inductive coupling. A transmitter coil generates an oscillating magnetic field; a receiver coil tuned to the same resonant frequency picks up this energy and uses it to light an LED. This is the principle behind wireless phone chargers and contactless power transfer.

PROJECT 05 Resonant Inductive Wireless Power Transfer ADVANCED

A BC547 transistor forms a Colpitts oscillator with the transmitter coil and capacitor. This oscillates at the LC resonant frequency (typically 100kHz–1MHz). The oscillating current in the transmitter coil creates an alternating magnetic field. A second coil (receiver), tuned to the same frequency, has an EMF induced in it by mutual inductance. A rectifier diode and filter capacitor convert the AC output to DC — powering the LED without any physical connection.

BC547 Transistor Transmitter Coil (10–15 turns, 8cm dia) Receiver Coil (10 turns, 6cm dia) 100nF Capacitor (tank circuit) 1kΩ Resistor (bias) 1N4148 Diode (rectifier) 10µF Capacitor (filter) LED + 470Ω Resistor 9V Battery
⚙️ HOW IT WORKS
1
Oscillator: The BC547 transistor, biased by the 1kΩ resistor, forms a self-sustaining LC oscillator with the transmitter coil (L1) and capacitor (100nF). The circuit oscillates at f = 1/(2π√LC). With L=10µH and C=100nF: f ≈ 159 kHz.
2
Magnetic Field Generation: The oscillating collector current surges back and forth through the transmitter coil at 159 kHz — creating a rapidly alternating magnetic field that radiates outward from the coil.
3
Inductive Coupling: The receiver coil (L2), placed within a few centimeters, is in this alternating magnetic field. By Faraday's Law, the changing field induces an AC voltage in L2. When L2 is tuned to the same frequency (same LC product), resonance maximizes energy transfer efficiency dramatically.
4
Rectification & Smoothing: The induced AC output is converted to DC by the 1N4148 rectifier diode. The 10µF capacitor smooths the pulsating DC output. This DC then drives the LED through a 470Ω current-limiting resistor.
5
Range & Efficiency: Efficiency drops sharply with distance. Maximum transfer occurs when coils are coaxially aligned and separated by less than their diameter. Increase coil diameter or turns to extend range. Qi wireless charging (phone pads) uses the same principle at 100–205 kHz.
📡 Coil Winding Tips: Wind both coils with 0.5mm enameled copper wire. Transmitter: 10 turns, 8cm diameter. Receiver: 15 turns, 6cm diameter. Keep coils parallel and aligned on the same axis for maximum coupling. Secure windings with tape so they don't unravel and change inductance.
🌐 Real-World Use: This circuit demonstrates the principle behind Qi wireless charging, RFID/NFC cards, inductive stovetops, electric toothbrush chargers, and implanted medical device charging — all using variants of resonant inductive coupling.
