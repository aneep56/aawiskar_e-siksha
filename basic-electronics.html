LESSON 1

What is Electricity?

Electricity is a form of energy caused by the movement of tiny particles called electrons. It powers everything around us — from light bulbs to smartphones — and understanding it starts with one simple question: what makes electrons move?

ELECTRONS FLOWING THROUGH A WIRE
💡 SIMPLE ANALOGY — WATER IN A PIPE
🚰 Water = Electric Charge
Just like water consists of water molecules, electricity consists of electrons. Both flow from one place to another and can do useful work.
🏔️ Water Tower = Battery
A water tower stores water and creates pressure. A battery stores chemical energy and creates electrical pressure (called Voltage).
🚿 Water Flow = Current
How much water flows per second through a pipe is like current — the number of electrons passing a point per second.
🪵 Narrow Pipe = Resistance
A narrow pipe slows water down. Resistance slows electrons down. Materials like copper have low resistance; rubber has very high resistance.
📖 Grade 5–6: Think of electricity as "invisible water" that flows through wires. The battery is the pump. The wire is the pipe. The light bulb is the water wheel that uses the flow to make energy!
🌍 WHERE DO WE USE ELECTRICITY?
  • Light: LED bulbs, tube lights, streetlights — electricity excites electrons in materials to produce photons (light particles).
  • Heat: Electric stoves, geysers, irons — electricity flows through high-resistance materials generating heat (Joule heating).
  • Motion: Electric fans, motors, trains — electricity creates magnetic fields that produce mechanical motion.
  • Sound: Speakers, buzzers, headphones — electricity makes a membrane vibrate, creating sound waves.
  • Information: Phones, computers, internet — very small electrical signals carry coded data at nearly the speed of light.

Atoms & Electrons

Everything in the universe — including you, this screen, and the wires in the wall — is made of atoms. Understanding the structure of an atom is the foundation of understanding why electricity exists.

COPPER ATOM — 29 ELECTRONS (SIMPLIFIED)

LESSON 3

Conductors & Insulators

Not all materials allow electricity to flow through them. Conductors let current flow freely. Insulators block current. A third class — semiconductors — can do both depending on conditions. This property is called electrical conductivity.

CONDUCTOR vs INSULATOR — ELECTRON BEHAVIOR
📊 CONDUCTOR vs SEMICONDUCTOR vs INSULATOR
PropertyConductorSemiconductorInsulator
Free electronsMany (very free)Few (controllable)Almost none
ResistivityVery lowMediumVery high
ExamplesCopper, Silver, Gold, AluminiumSilicon, GermaniumRubber, Glass, Plastic, Air, Wood
Used forWires, connectorsTransistors, solar cells, ICsWire insulation, cases
Can be controlled?NoYes (by doping, heat, light)No
🧲 SPECIAL CASES TO KNOW
  • Carbon (graphite): A non-metal that IS a conductor! Used in pencil "lead" and electrodes. Carbon has a special bonding structure that lets electrons move freely.
  • Water: Pure water is an insulator. But water with dissolved salts (like seawater or tap water) is a conductor — the ions carry charge. This is why you should NEVER touch electrical things with wet hands.
  • Superconductors: Some materials at very low temperatures (near −273°C) have ZERO resistance. Current flows forever with no energy loss!
  • Doped semiconductors: By adding tiny amounts of other elements (called "dopants") to silicon, we can make it conduct or not — this is how transistors and computer chips work.
LESSON 4

Static Electricity

Static electricity happens when electric charges build up on a surface but don't flow anywhere — they are "static" (not moving). When the built-up charge suddenly discharges, you feel a spark or see lightning!

STATIC CHARGE BUILDUP — BALLOON & HAIR
⚡ HOW STATIC ELECTRICITY BUILDS UP
  • Friction: When two materials rub together, electrons transfer from one to the other. The material that gains electrons becomes negatively charged; the one that loses them becomes positive.
  • Induction: A charged object brought near a neutral object can attract or repel charges without touching — this temporarily charges the neutral object.
  • Discharge: When the built-up charge gets large enough, electrons jump through air to equalize the difference — this is a spark or lightning bolt.
⚡ Lightning Facts: A single lightning bolt can reach 30,000 Kelvin (5× hotter than the sun's surface!) and carries about 1 billion Volts of potential difference. It lasts only 0.2 seconds but transfers enormous energy.
📱 Everyday Static: Rubbing a comb on your hair, touching a door handle after walking on carpet, clothes clinging in a dryer, balloon sticking to a wall — all static electricity!
LESSON 5

Electrical Safety

Electricity is incredibly useful but can be dangerous if mishandled. Even low voltages like 50V AC or the 230V from a wall socket can be lethal. These safety rules could save your life.

🛡️ CRITICAL SAFETY RULES — NEVER BREAK THESE
  • Never touch live wires with bare hands. Always switch off the mains before working on household wiring. In Nepal, household voltage is 230V AC — this WILL kill if you touch it.
  • Keep water away from electricity. Water (especially tap water with minerals) conducts electricity. Never touch switches, sockets, or devices with wet hands.
  • Don't insert objects into sockets. The holes in a wall socket are directly connected to high-voltage power lines. This is extremely dangerous.
  • Always use the correct fuse or circuit breaker. Fuses protect circuits by breaking if too much current flows. Using the wrong (higher) fuse removes this protection.
  • In the lab, work only below 30V DC. For school experiments, always use batteries (9V, 12V) or regulated power supplies. Never work directly with mains (230V).
  • Switch off before connecting/disconnecting components. Always power off your circuit before adding or removing wires or components to avoid short circuits or component damage.
⚠️ What to do if someone is being electrocuted: DO NOT touch them! Switch off the power immediately. If you can't switch off, use a non-conducting object (dry wood, plastic chair) to push them away from the live source. Call emergency services (100 in Nepal).

Voltage — Electrical Pressure

Voltage is the "electrical pressure" that pushes electrons through a circuit. Measured in Volts (V), it represents the difference in electrical potential energy between two points. Without voltage, electrons have no reason to move.

LESSON 2

Current — Electron Flow

Electric current is the rate at which electric charge flows past a point in a circuit. Measured in Amperes (A), it tells you how many electrons are moving per second. One Ampere = 6.24 × 10¹⁸ electrons per second!

📊 CURRENT UNITS
UnitSymbolValueUsed For
AmpereA1 ALarge loads — motors, heating elements
MilliamperemA0.001 A (1/1000 A)Most electronic circuits — LEDs, transistors
MicroampereµA0.000001 ASensors, microcontrollers in sleep mode
⚠️ Body Danger: It's not Voltage that kills directly — it's Current. As little as 10mA through your heart can cause ventricular fibrillation (irregular heartbeat), and 100mA can be fatal. The reason high voltage is dangerous is because it drives large current through your body's resistance.
📖 Conventional vs Electron Current: Historically, scientists assumed current flowed from + to −. Later, electrons were discovered to actually flow from − to +. We still use "conventional current" (+ to −) in circuit diagrams by convention — be aware of this difference!
LESSON 3

Resistance — Opposition to Flow

Every material opposes the flow of electrons to some degree. This opposition is called Resistance, measured in Ohms (Ω). Higher resistance means less current flows for the same voltage. Resistance converts electrical energy into heat.

HIGH vs LOW RESISTANCE — PIPE WIDTH ANALOGY
🌡️ FACTORS THAT AFFECT RESISTANCE
  • Material (Resistivity ρ): Copper has very low resistivity (1.7×10⁻⁸ Ω·m). Nichrome (heating element wire) has 100× more. Each material has a fixed resistivity value.
  • Length (L): Longer wire = more resistance. Resistance is directly proportional to length: R ∝ L. Double the length → double the resistance.
  • Cross-sectional Area (A): Thicker wire = less resistance. R ∝ 1/A. Double the thickness → half the resistance.
  • Temperature: For most metals, resistance increases with temperature (positive temperature coefficient). For semiconductors like silicon, resistance DECREASES with temperature.
R = ρ × L / A
ρ = material resistivity (Ω·m) | L = wire length (m) | A = cross-sectional area (m²)
Grade 9–10: This explains why power lines use thick aluminium cables!
LESSON 4

Ohm's Law

Ohm's Law is the most fundamental equation in electronics. It states that the voltage across a component equals the current through it multiplied by its resistance. Discovered by Georg Ohm in 1827, it governs every resistive circuit.

V = I × R
V = Voltage in Volts  |  I = Current in Amperes  |  R = Resistance in Ohms
Rearrange:   I = V ÷ R    |    R = V ÷ I

LESSON 5

Power Formula

Electrical power is the rate at which electrical energy is converted to other forms (heat, light, motion). Measured in Watts (W), it tells you how much energy a component uses per second. Your electricity bill is based on power usage over time!

P = V × I   =   I² × R   =   V² ÷ R
P = Power (Watts) | V = Voltage (V) | I = Current (A) | R = Resistance (Ω)
1 Watt = 1 Joule of energy per second | 1 kWh = 1000W running for 1 hour

LESSON 1

Open Circuit

An open circuit has a break (gap) somewhere in the path. Since there is no complete loop, electrons cannot flow — no current, no light, no function. The component is effectively disconnected from the power source.

OPEN CIRCUIT — SWITCH OPEN, NO CURRENT
🔍 CAUSES OF OPEN CIRCUITS
  • Open switch: Deliberately breaking the circuit to stop current — a light switch, for example.
  • Broken wire: A wire that has been cut or has snapped internally. Current cannot cross the gap.
  • Blown fuse: The fuse wire melts when excess current passes through, deliberately creating an open circuit to protect the circuit.
  • Loose connection: A component leg not properly seated in a breadboard hole. Looks connected but isn't making contact.
  • Burned out component: A bulb's filament or an LED that has blown open internally.
LESSON 2

Closed Circuit

A closed circuit is a complete, unbroken loop from the positive terminal, through all components, back to the negative terminal. Current flows, and components do their job — lights glow, motors spin, buzzers beep.

CLOSED CIRCUIT — SWITCH CLOSED, CURRENT FLOWS
LESSON 3

Series Circuit

In a series circuit, all components are connected end-to-end in a single loop. There is exactly one path for electrons to travel. The same current flows through every component, but voltage divides proportionally across each one.

SERIES CIRCUIT — SAME CURRENT, VOLTAGE DIVIDES
📐 SERIES CIRCUIT RULES
Rtotal = R₁ + R₂ + R₃ + …
Current is SAME everywhere: I = V_total ÷ R_total
Voltages ADD UP: V₁ + V₂ + V₃ = V_source (Kirchhoff's Voltage Law)
  • Voltage Divider: Each resistor "grabs" a portion of the total voltage proportional to its resistance. V₁ = I × R₁, V₂ = I × R₂, etc.
  • Failure mode: If any component opens (breaks), the ENTIRE circuit stops. This was the problem with old Christmas lights!
  • Grade 9–10 — Kirchhoff's Voltage Law (KVL): The sum of all voltage drops around a closed loop equals the source voltage. This is conservation of energy applied to circuits.
LESSON 4

Parallel Circuit

In a parallel circuit, components are connected across the same two nodes, providing multiple independent paths for current. Each branch operates independently — same voltage across all branches, current divides between them.

PARALLEL CIRCUIT — SAME VOLTAGE, CURRENT SPLITS
📐 PARALLEL CIRCUIT RULES
1/Rtotal = 1/R₁ + 1/R₂ + 1/R₃
Voltage is SAME across all branches: V₁ = V₂ = V₃ = V_source
Currents ADD UP: I_total = I₁ + I₂ + I₃ (Kirchhoff's Current Law)
  • Independent operation: If one branch fails (opens), the others continue working. This is why household wiring is parallel — your fan works even if a bulb blows.
  • Total resistance decreases: Adding branches gives more paths, reducing total resistance. For N equal resistors of value R: R_total = R/N.
  • Higher current demand: More branches means more total current drawn from the source. Too many parallel loads can blow a fuse.
🏠 Home wiring is parallel: Every socket in your house is connected in parallel. Each device gets the full 230V. Turning one off doesn't affect others. Adding more devices increases total current — which is why circuit breakers are essential.
LESSON 5

Mixed (Series-Parallel) Circuits

Real-world circuits are rarely pure series or pure parallel — they are combinations of both. Analyzing mixed circuits requires breaking them into recognizable series and parallel sections and solving step by step.

🔢 HOW TO SOLVE MIXED CIRCUITS (GRADE 9–10)
  • Step 1 — Identify parallel groups: Find components sharing the same two nodes (same start and end points). These are in parallel.
  • Step 2 — Simplify parallel groups: Calculate equivalent resistance for each parallel group using 1/R_eq = 1/R₁ + 1/R₂...
  • Step 3 — Redraw the circuit: Replace each parallel group with its single equivalent resistor. Now you have a simpler series circuit.
  • Step 4 — Solve the series circuit: Add all remaining resistances. Use Ohm's Law to find total current.
  • Step 5 — Work backwards: Use the total current and the equivalent resistances to find voltages and currents in each original branch.
Example: R1 (100Ω) in series with a parallel group of R2 (200Ω) ∥ R3 (200Ω).
Step 1: R2∥R3 = 100Ω. Step 2: R_total = R1 + 100 = 200Ω. Step 3: I_total = 9V ÷ 200Ω = 45mA. Step 4: V_R1 = 45mA × 100 = 4.5V, V_parallel_group = 4.5V. Step 5: I₂ = I₃ = 4.5V ÷ 200 = 22.5mA each.

LESSON 2

Capacitors — Charge & Discharge

A capacitor stores electrical energy in an electric field between two conductive plates separated by an insulator (dielectric). Unlike a battery, it charges and discharges very quickly — in milliseconds. Measured in Farads (F).

CAPACITOR CHARGING — PLATES ACCUMULATE CHARGE
🔵 CAPACITOR KEY FACTS
C = Q / V    |    E = ½ × C × V²
C = Capacitance (Farads) | Q = Charge stored (Coulombs) | V = Voltage across capacitor
E = Energy stored (Joules) — charge a 1000µF cap to 12V → stores 0.072J of energy
  • Blocks DC, passes AC: Once fully charged, a capacitor blocks DC (no more current flows). But AC alternates direction — the cap continuously charges and discharges, effectively "passing" AC signals.
  • Time constant (τ = RC): How long it takes to charge to ~63% of supply voltage. τ = R × C. At 5τ it's essentially fully charged. (Grade 9–10)
  • Electrolytic capacitors are polarized: They have a + and − leg. Connect the + leg to the higher voltage. Reverse connection can damage or even explode the capacitor!
  • Applications: Power supply filtering (smoothing voltage), coupling (passing AC between amplifier stages), decoupling (removing noise from power supply), timing circuits, camera flash storage.

LESSON 3

Inductors — Coils & Magnetism

An inductor is a coil of wire that stores energy in a magnetic field when current flows through it. It opposes changes in current. Measured in Henries (H), inductors are key components in power converters, filters, and radio circuits.

INDUCTOR — CURRENT CREATES MAGNETIC FIELD
🌀 HOW AN INDUCTOR WORKS
  • Electromagnetic induction: When current flows through a coil, it creates a magnetic field. When current changes, the changing field induces a voltage in the coil that opposes the change (Lenz's Law).
  • Passes DC, blocks AC: Opposite to a capacitor! DC passes through freely (just the wire resistance). High-frequency AC is blocked because the inductor continuously opposes the changing current.
  • Energy storage: Stored energy E = ½LI². When the current is cut, the inductor releases this energy — this can cause a large voltage spike that damages transistors! (Always use a flyback diode.)
  • Applications: Power supply chokes, LC oscillators (radio tuning), transformers, relay coils, motor windings, wireless charging coils.
📡 Radio Tuning: In an LC (Inductor-Capacitor) circuit, the resonant frequency is f = 1 / (2π√LC). By adjusting L or C, you tune to different radio stations — this was the basis of all early radio receivers!
LESSON 4

Transformers

A transformer uses electromagnetic induction to transfer electrical energy between two coils (primary and secondary) without direct electrical contact. It can step voltage UP or DOWN while conserving power. The basis of all AC power distribution.

📐 TRANSFORMER FORMULA
V_primary / V_secondary = N_primary / N_secondary
N = number of turns in each coil | V = voltage | I = current
Also: I_primary / I_secondary = N_secondary / N_primary (power is conserved: P_in ≈ P_out)
  • Step-up transformer: More turns in secondary than primary. Increases voltage, decreases current. Used in power transmission (11kV → 132kV) to reduce I²R losses in long cables.
  • Step-down transformer: More turns in primary. Reduces voltage. Your phone charger has a step-down transformer converting 230V to 5–20V.
  • Only works with AC: A transformer needs changing magnetic flux, so it only works with alternating current. DC produces a static field — no induction.
Example: Primary has 2300 turns at 230V. Secondary has 100 turns. Secondary voltage = 230 × (100/2300) = 10V. If primary draws 0.1A, secondary current = 0.1 × (2300/100) = 2.3A. Power in = Power out = 23W.

LESSON 3

Transistors — BC547 NPN

A transistor is a semiconductor amplifier and switching device with three terminals: Base (B), Collector (C), and Emitter (E). A small current at the Base controls a much larger current from Collector to Emitter — making it the core of all modern electronics.

NPN TRANSISTOR — BASE SIGNAL CONTROLS COLLECTOR LOAD
🔺 BC547 NPN TRANSISTOR — PINOUT & SPECS
Flat Side Facing You
Left pin = Collector, Middle pin = Base, Right pin = Emitter. Always check datasheet — some transistors have different pinouts!
Current Gain (β or hFE)
BC547 has β = 100–800. This means 1mA of base current can control up to 800mA of collector current. For safe operation, use a base resistor.
Switching Mode
If V_BE > 0.6V, transistor turns ON (saturates). If V_BE < 0.6V, transistor is OFF (cut-off). Used to switch LEDs, buzzers, relays.
Amplification Mode
In the active region, I_C = β × I_B. Used in audio amplifiers, sensor circuits. The transistor acts as a current-controlled current source.
β = I_C / I_B    |    R_base = (V_supply − 0.6) / I_B
To fully switch ON an LED (I_C = 20mA) with β=200, you need I_B = 20/200 = 0.1mA minimum.
R_base = (9 − 0.6) / 0.001 = 8.4kΩ → use 10kΩ (gives headroom for reliable saturation)

LESSON 1

LDR — Light Dependent Resistor

An LDR (also called a photoresistor) changes its resistance based on light intensity. In darkness, resistance is very high (1MΩ). In bright light, resistance drops drastically (100–500Ω). Used in automatic lights, cameras, and solar trackers.

LDR — RESISTANCE CHANGES WITH LIGHT
🌙 LDR VOLTAGE DIVIDER CIRCUIT (Used in Auto Night Light)
V_out = V_cc × R_fixed / (R_LDR + R_fixed)
In darkness: R_LDR=1MΩ, R_fixed=10kΩ → V_out = 9 × 10k/(1010k) ≈ 0.09V (LOW)
In light: R_LDR=300Ω, R_fixed=10kΩ → V_out = 9 × 10k/(10.3k) ≈ 8.74V (HIGH)
This voltage change triggers a transistor to switch a load ON or OFF!
⚡ In auto night light: Dark → R_LDR high → V_out low → transistor OFF → LED OFF.
Bright → R_LDR low → V_out high → transistor base activated → LED ON.
Reverse the component order to get light-activated vs dark-activated behavior.
LESSON 2

Thermistor — Temperature Sensor

A thermistor is a resistor whose value changes significantly with temperature. NTC (Negative Temperature Coefficient) thermistors decrease in resistance as temperature rises. PTC (Positive) increases. Used in thermometers, temperature controls, and motor protection.

🌡️ NTC vs PTC THERMISTORS

Steinhart-Hart Equation (Grade 9–10): The relationship between temperature and NTC thermistor resistance is: 1/T = A + B·ln(R) + C·ln(R)³. For engineering accuracy, always calibrate your thermistor against a reference thermometer at known temperatures.
  • Voltage divider circuit: Connect thermistor in series with a known resistor. Read the voltage at the midpoint. As temperature changes, so does the voltage — which you can measure or feed to a comparator.
  • Fire alarm: NTC thermistor in a circuit — when fire heats it, R drops, voltage changes, triggering an alarm transistor.
  • Body temperature: NTC thermistors sensitive to 0.01°C are used in digital clinical thermometers.

LESSON 4

DC Motor

A DC motor converts electrical energy into rotational mechanical energy using electromagnetic principles. When current flows through the armature coil inside a magnetic field, a force (Lorentz force) causes rotation. Reversing polarity reverses rotation direction.

DC MOTOR — ROTATING ARMATURE
⚙️ KEY DC MOTOR FACTS
  • Speed control: PWM (Pulse Width Modulation) — rapidly switching the motor ON and OFF at varying duty cycles. 50% duty = half speed. This is how an Arduino controls motor speed.
  • Direction control: Use an H-Bridge circuit (like the L298N chip) to reverse polarity electronically without rewiring. Essential for robots.
  • Back-EMF protection: When motor is switched OFF, the spinning armature generates a voltage that can damage transistors. Always place a flyback diode across the motor terminals.
  • Stall current: When a motor is held stationary and voltage applied, it draws maximum current (stall current) — far more than running current. Always rate your driver circuit for stall current.
LESSON 5

Relay — Electromagnetic Switch

A relay is an electrically operated mechanical switch. A small current through the electromagnet coil (control circuit) creates a magnetic field that pulls a metal armature, mechanically closing or opening a separate high-power contact (load circuit).

🔀 RELAY TYPES AND CONTACTS
TerminalMeaningFunction

Why use a relay? Your Arduino outputs 3.3–5V at very low current. A relay lets this tiny signal switch 230V AC household appliances safely — the control circuit and load circuit are COMPLETELY isolated. This is critical for mains-powered projects.
⚠️ Always use a flyback diode across the relay coil (cathode to +V, anode to the transistor side). When the coil is de-energized, it releases a large voltage spike that can destroy your transistor — the diode provides a safe path for this spike to dissipate.

LESSON 2

Breadboard

A breadboard (prototype board) allows you to build and test circuits without soldering. Components and wires plug into the holes. Internal metal strips connect the holes in a specific pattern. Understanding this internal wiring is essential.

BREADBOARD INTERNAL CONNECTIONS
🧱 GOLDEN RULES FOR BREADBOARD USE
  • IC chips go across the center divider: Each pin sits in a separate row on each side of the divider, allowing you to connect individual pins without shorting them.
  • Power rails are for V+ and GND only: Connect your battery + to the red rail, − to the blue rail. Then use short jumpers to bring power to your component rows.
  • Always wire before powering: Build the complete circuit, then apply power. Diagnose any issues before adding voltage to avoid damage.
  • Color-code your wires: Use RED for positive/V+, BLACK for GND, and other colors for signal lines. This makes debugging dramatically easier.
  • Check component orientation: LEDs, capacitors, diodes, and transistors have polarity/pinout. Insert them in the correct orientation before powering up.

LESSON 4

PCB Basics

A PCB (Printed Circuit Board) is a flat board made of fiberglass (FR4) with copper "traces" that replace wires. Components are soldered onto the board. PCBs allow complex circuits to be miniaturized, mass-produced, and made reliable.

🟢 ANATOMY OF A PCB

Designing your first PCB: Draw schematic in KiCad (free) → Assign footprints to components → Use PCB editor to place components and route copper traces → Export Gerber files → Submit to PCB manufacturer (JLCPCB, PCBWay — 5 boards for ~$2 shipping to Nepal).
  • Through-hole (THT) vs Surface-mount (SMD): THT components have long legs that go through holes and are soldered on the back. SMD components sit on pads on the surface — much smaller, harder to hand-solder, used in modern commercial electronics.

PROJECT 1

Auto Night Light

Build a circuit that turns an LED ON automatically in darkness and turns it OFF in bright light — using an LDR, a transistor, and a few resistors. This is the fundamental principle behind streetlights, staircase lights, and solar garden lights.

PROJECT 01
🌙 LDR Auto Night Light
BEGINNER
Grade 7+
The LDR forms a voltage divider with R1. In the dark, R_LDR is very high — the voltage at the base of Q1 (BC547) is LOW, turning the transistor OFF and the LED OFF. When it's bright, R_LDR drops, the base voltage rises above 0.6V, transistor turns ON, and the LED glows. Adjusting R1 sets the light sensitivity threshold.

🔧 STEP-BY-STEP BUILD
1
Insert BC547: Place the transistor across the center divider of your breadboard. Identify legs: flat face pointing toward you — left = Collector, middle = Base, right = Emitter.
2
LDR + R1 voltage divider: Connect one end of the LDR to the + power rail. Connect the other end of LDR to one end of R1 (10kΩ). Connect the other end of R1 to GND rail. The junction between LDR and R1 is your Base voltage point.

💡 KEY CONCEPT: VOLTAGE DIVIDER AS SENSOR INPUT

The LDR and R1 form a voltage divider: V_base = 9V × R1 / (R_LDR + R1). In darkness, R_LDR ≈ 1MΩ, so V_base ≈ 0.09V — below the 0.6V threshold. In bright light, R_LDR ≈ 1kΩ, so V_base ≈ 8.2V — well above threshold, saturating the transistor. This divider principle is used in almost every analog sensor circuit.

Touch Alarm

Build a circuit that triggers a buzzer the moment you touch two exposed wires. Your body's resistance (~100kΩ) acts as a base resistor for a transistor, completing the circuit. Used in anti-tamper security, burglar alarms, and touch-activated displays.

PROJECT 02
👆 Human-Touch Alarm

Grade 7+
When you bridge the two sensor wires with your fingertips, your skin resistance (~50kΩ–200kΩ) provides just enough base current to saturate the BC547 NPN transistor. The collector circuit drives a piezo buzzer. No touch = open base = transistor OFF = silence. Touch = transistor ON = alarm sounds.

🔧 STEP-BY-STEP BUILD
1
Prepare sensor probes: Strip two 5cm pieces of wire. These are your touch sensor leads — they should be bare copper at the tips, spaced about 1cm apart on your board (or taped to a cardboard surface).

Water Level Indicator

💧 3-Level Water Detector
INTERMEDIATE

When water rises to a probe pair, water's conductivity (~200–500µS/cm for tap water) closes the circuit, turning ON the corresponding BC547 transistor and lighting its LED. 3× BC547 Transistors (Q1, Q2, Q3)

1

Pure water is actually a poor conductor (~18MΩ·cm resistivity). PROJECT 4

Temperature Alert

Build a circuit that triggers an alarm when temperature rises above a set threshold — using an NTC thermistor, a comparator voltage divider, and a buzzer. This is the core principle in fire alarms, refrigerator monitors, and engine overheat protection systems.

PROJECT 04
🌡️ Thermal Threshold Alarm
INTERMEDIATE
Grade 8+
An NTC thermistor (resistance decreases as temperature rises) forms a voltage divider with a fixed resistor R1. The output of this divider feeds the Base of a BC547 transistor. At normal temperature, the thermistor resistance is high → low base voltage → transistor OFF → buzzer silent. When it gets hot, thermistor resistance drops → base voltage rises → transistor turns ON → buzzer sounds. The threshold is tuned by adjusting R1 or using a potentiometer.
9V Battery
NTC Thermistor (10kΩ at 25°C)
BC547 NPN Transistor
10kΩ Potentiometer (threshold adjust)
Active Piezo Buzzer
470Ω Resistor (base protection)
Red LED + 470Ω (optional visual indicator)
Breadboard + Jumpers
🔧 STEP-BY-STEP BUILD
1
Wire the voltage divider: Connect one end of the NTC thermistor to + rail. Other end connects to one terminal of the 10kΩ potentiometer. The other pot terminal goes to GND. The wiper (middle pin) of the pot is your output voltage node.
2
Base connection: Connect the wiper (output node) through a 470Ω resistor to the Base of BC547. This 470Ω protects the transistor from excess base current.

💡 KEY CONCEPT: NEGATIVE TEMPERATURE COEFFICIENT (NTC)

An NTC thermistor has resistance that drops exponentially with temperature, described by the Steinhart–Hart equation. A typical 10kΩ NTC thermistor at 25°C drops to ~3.5kΩ at 50°C and ~1.5kΩ at 70°C. Combined with a fixed 10kΩ R1: at 25°C, V_base = 9 × 10/(10+10) = 4.5V. This simple divider principle is used in virtually every appliance with a temperature sensor — washing machines, ovens, car engines, and air conditioners all use NTC thermistors with comparator circuits.

PROJECT 5

Wireless Power Transmission

Transmit power wirelessly using resonant inductive coupling — the same technology in your phone's wireless charger. A transmitter coil driven by an oscillator creates a changing magnetic field; a receiver coil picks up this energy and lights an LED, with no physical connection.

PROJECT 05
📡 Resonant Inductive Coupling
ADVANCED
Grade 9+
A simple transistor-based oscillator (Colpitts-like circuit) drives the TX coil at its resonant frequency. When the RX coil is tuned to the same frequency and brought within range, mutual inductance transfers energy across the air gap. A rectifier diode and smoothing capacitor on the RX side convert the AC induced voltage into DC to power an LED. Range is limited by coil size and coupling coefficient.
9V Battery
2N2222 or BC547 Transistor (oscillator)
TX Coil: 20 turns, 6cm diameter, 26AWG enameled wire
RX Coil: 20 turns, 6cm diameter, 26AWG enameled wire
1N4148 Diode (rectifier)
100µF Capacitor (smoothing)
2× 100nF Ceramic Capacitors (tank circuit)
10kΩ Resistor (base bias)
Red LED
MAGNETIC FIELD COUPLING BETWEEN COILS
🔧 STEP-BY-STEP BUILD
1
Wind both coils: Wrap 20 turns of 26AWG enameled copper wire around a 6cm circular former (a pill bottle or PVC pipe works well). Leave 10cm tails for connections. Scrape the enamel off both ends with sandpaper to expose bare copper. Wind RX coil identically.
2

3

💡 KEY CONCEPT: MUTUAL INDUCTANCE & RESONANCE

Mutual inductance M (measured in henries, H) describes how much of coil TX's changing magnetic flux links with coil RX. The induced EMF in RX = M × (dI/dt). Resonance occurs when the oscillator frequency equals 1 / (2π√LC) of the tank circuit — at resonance, even small M can transfer significant energy. Qi wireless charging (used in smartphones) extends this to 5–15W at up to 12mm, operating at 110–205 kHz. Larger systems (electric vehicle charging pads) use the same principle at up to 11kW.

🔬 THE ENGINEERING DESIGN PROCESS
STEP 1 — DEFINE
State the problem clearly: "I want a circuit that does X when Y happens." Who uses it? What are the success conditions?
STEP 2 — RESEARCH
Which sensors detect your input? Which components control your output? Look up datasheets and similar projects to understand what's possible.
STEP 3 — DESIGN
Draw the schematic on paper first. Label all component values. Calculate resistor values using Ohm's law. Predict what happens at each node.
STEP 4 — PROTOTYPE
Build on breadboard. Test each sub-circuit independently before connecting them together. Use multimeter to verify voltages at each stage.
STEP 5 — TEST
Does it do what you said? Test all edge cases. What happens at minimum and maximum sensor values? What if power varies? Document failures honestly.
STEP 6 — ITERATE
Almost nothing works perfectly on the first try. Identify what failed, understand why, change one variable at a time, and test again. This is real engineering.
